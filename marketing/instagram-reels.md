**Visual:** Split screen, three patterns animating:
- Left: Coherent (5-5)
- Middle: Box (4-4-4-4)
- Right: 4-7-8

Each with a one-line use case overlay.

**Audio:** Voice-over decision tree.

### Caption

Three breathing techniques every couple should know — and exactly when to use each.

**Coherent breathing (5-5)** — Daily morning ritual. Maxes heart rate variability at exactly 0.1 Hz (Lehrer's HRV biofeedback research). Easiest to sync with a partner. This is your default.

**Box breathing (4-4-4-4)** — Before a hard conversation. Used by Navy SEALs for performance under pressure. Equal phases feel "fair" — useful if either of you tends to dominate the rhythm.

**4-7-8 breathing** — Anxiety, sleep, post-fight repair. Andrew Weil's protocol — the long exhale gives strong vagal activation. Knocks the nervous system out of fight-or-flight fastest.

Decision tree:
- Morning routine → coherent
- About to talk about something hard → box
- One of you is anxious or can't sleep → 4-7-8
- Recovering from a fight → 4-7-8 → transition to coherent

## Reel 4 — "Your partner walks in stressed. You catch it within 5 minutes. Here's why."

**Visual:** Two people on a split screen — one stressed, one calm at start. Stress visibly transfers. Text overlay: "This is co-regulation working in reverse."

**Audio:** "You haven't said a word. You haven't even looked at each other. But within five minutes you're tense too. That's not coincidence. That's your nervous system mirroring theirs through subtle cues — micro-expressions, breath rhythm, posture. Co-regulation runs both directions. The good news: it's trainable."

### Caption

Your partner walks in stressed. Five minutes later you're tense too. You haven't said a word.

This is co-regulation working in reverse — what some clinicians call "limbic resonance" or co-dysregulation. Your nervous system reads your partner's state through micro-cues: breath rhythm, posture, voice prosody, micro-expressions. Mirror neurons fire. Within minutes you're dysregulating each other.

The fix isn't to stop being affected — that's not how attachment works. The fix is to *deliberately* practice the calm direction of co-regulation, daily, when nothing is wrong. Build the pathway when calm, and it's available when it's not.

This is why daily synchronized breathing isn't just "nice." It's reps. You're training your two-person nervous system to default toward calm. Then, when one of you walks in stressed, the dominant state in the field is your shared parasympathetic baseline — not their cortisol.

10 minutes. Every morning. Before the day pulls you apart.
