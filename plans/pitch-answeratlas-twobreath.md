## ElevenLabs production brief — our own voices in three languages

**The craft move:** every other team at this hackathon will use English text-to-speech. We use ElevenLabs to speak our own voices **in Japanese and German, natively accented, by cloning Matthi and Dascha's voices**. The jury sees the founders telling the story themselves — not in their mother tongue, but in the mother tongue of the couple they are reaching.

### Step 1 — Voice cloning (Saturday evening, 30 min total)

- Record ~3 minutes of each of us reading a neutral English passage (a news article works). 48 kHz / 24-bit mono, quiet room, pop filter.
- Upload to **ElevenLabs Voice Lab** → Instant Voice Cloning. Tag as `matthi-01` and `dascha-01`. Preserve them for the app itself later — these voices will narrate every morning ritual in twobreath eventually.
- Test quality on the English script first to confirm the clones land.

### Step 2 — Multilingual generation (Saturday night, 1 hour)

- Use **ElevenLabs Multilingual v2** model with the cloned voices.
- Language target for the demo line: **Japanese (ja)**. Secondary target for the extended cut: **German (de)**.
- Paste the translated-and-culturally-adapted lines from Gemini (not a literal translation — see Image Brief for the Japanese line; see Market Focus section for German variants).
- Render multiple takes per line. Pick the take where the pacing matches the breathing rhythm — slower than conversational, measured, with a beat between sentences.

### Step 3 — Post (Sunday morning, 30 min)

- Mix ElevenLabs output at -6 dB under the English master, EQ-match to the original recordings so the Japanese and German lines feel like our real voices, not a dubbed overlay.
- Add a 0.5-second breath cue before each multilingual clip (taken from the 5-6 bpm breath track) so the cadence stays continuous when language switches.

## Market focus — US first, Japan second, Germany third

The three-market order is deliberate. Peec AI's data lets us rank launch priority by **uncontested prompt density × longevity-community concentration**, not by GDP.

### 🇺🇸 #1 — United States (weeks 1-4 of the 90-day plan)

**Why first:** the English-speaking longevity community is the largest single audience we reach, and almost every inbound link we later need for Japanese and German citation authority will originate in English-language coverage first.

- **Who we target:** the audiences of Peter Attia (*Outlive*, *The Drive* podcast), Andrew Huberman (*Huberman Lab*), Bryan Johnson (*Don't Die*), Zone 2 + HRV Twitter. These are people already tracking their heart-rate variability on an Apple Watch every morning. Every one of them is in a relationship. None of them have a protocol for it.
- **Prompt territories Peec shows as uncontested:** "breathing app for couples," "apple watch hrv couple sync," "longevity protocol for relationships," "gottman daily ritual app," "arthur brooks happiness hygiene app."
- **Citation graph:** Reddit (r/PeterAttia, r/AppleWatch, r/longevity), The Verge, Wirecutter wellness, YouTube commentary channels in the Attia/Huberman orbit, Substack writers on HRV and relationships.
- **Creative frame:** performance + longevity. "The twenty minutes with your partner that changes the next ten years of your relationship." Evidence-forward. HRV graphs and Gottman citations.

### 🇯🇵 #2 — Japan (weeks 3-6, overlapping with US push)

**Why second, and why second matters:** the Japanese market is the most culturally aligned with paired daily practice (二人, paired harmony; 整える, tuning oneself) and simultaneously the **least commercially served** by existing Western wellness apps. Peec's data shows English wellness app citations barely cross into Japanese AI answers. The field is empty. Being first-in-Japanese builds a moat no US-first competitor can copy.

- **Who we target:** the Japanese longevity community (which exists and is growing — Okinawa, Blue Zones, *ikigai* adjacent wellness, the Note.com wellness-writer ecosystem). Couples in their 30s-40s in Tokyo, Osaka, Fukuoka who know that their relationship needs a daily practice the same way their body needs one.
- **Prompt territories Peec shows as essentially empty:** *二人でできる呼吸アプリ*, *夫婦 朝 ルーティン アプリ*, *パートナー HRV 同期*, *長寿 カップル 習慣*.
- **Citation graph:** Note.com (the dominant essay platform for wellness writers in Japan), 5ch wellness threads, Japanese App Store review aggregators, selected Japanese wellness Substacks.
- **Creative frame:** relationship-as-craft. *二人の呼吸を重ねる四分間* — "four minutes of overlapping breaths." Poetic, kuuki-aware, slower cadence. Not performance; practice.

### 🇩🇪 #3 — Germany (weeks 5-9)

**Why third:** smallest of the three markets for this category and most regulated, but the warmest receptive audience once the product has US + JP citation authority to reference.

- **Who we target:** German couples in the *Achtsamkeit + Stressbewältigung* category, Krankenkasse-eligible wellness seekers, the *heise* / *t3n* reader crossed with the Atem-therapy community.
- **Prompt territories Peec shows as partially contested:** *Atem-App für Paare*, *HRV Partner Apple Watch*, *Stressbewältigung gemeinsam*, *Atemtherapie Krankenkasse*.
- **Citation graph:** *heise*, *t3n*, selected German wellness Substacks, *Spektrum* adjacent popular-science sources when the Gottman / Brooks research is referenced.
- **Creative frame:** evidence-forward precision. *Vier Minuten gemeinsam atmen. Nachweislich niedrigerer Cortisolspiegel.* Calm German voice, peer-reviewed framing, Krankenkasse-eligibility mentioned explicitly.

## The longevity-community framing

**Why longevity and not wellness:**

Wellness is a saturated, price-sensitive, female-skewed category dominated by Calm and Headspace with hundreds of smaller players. Longevity is a fast-growing, high-intent, balanced-gender category centered on Peter Attia, Huberman, Bryan Johnson, and the HRV / Zone 2 / sleep-optimization community. Longevity buyers already spend $200-2,000 a year on supplements, CGMs, and wearables. They are looking for the next protocol. They do not yet have a relationship protocol.

**Our line:** twobreath is the first longevity protocol for couples. Every other longevity intervention is individual — Zone 2, fasting, sauna, cold plunge. Relationships compound over decades and predict lifespan outcomes (see the Harvard Study of Adult Development, 86 years running). Yet no one in the longevity community is selling a relationship protocol. We are the first.

**Why this framing helps us win Peec AI specifically:** the Peec team is judging commercial defensibility, not poetry. Reframing from "wellness" to "longevity" moves the twobreath positioning from a red ocean into an open blue one, and it does so with a citation path (Attia, Huberman, Gottman, Brooks) that Peec's data can already see.

## Community, not subscription — the €200-per-year problem we lived

For three years we paid subscription fees to breathing apps that were not built for two. Calm: $70/year/person. Headspace: $70/year/person. Othership: $100/year/person. Breathwrk: $90/year/person. Over three years across both of us and across trying every app we could find, we spent roughly **$600** to learn that none of these apps were ever going to be the one.

**We will not build twobreath as a $200-a-year per-couple subscription.** The business model has to match the value: a morning ritual that compounds over decades is not a utility you rent monthly. We are building twobreath as a **one-time purchase + a community layer** — a place where couples who share the practice share poems, custom breathwork patterns, and accountability. The community is the moat. The subscription is what we are refusing to build.

**Why this matters for the pitch:** a VC in the room will instinctively ask about ARR per couple. Our answer: community LTV and anti-churn through shared rituals. The anti-subscription stance **is** the story. Every couple in the audience has paid $200/year for Calm they stopped opening in week three.

## Why Peec AI is the indispensable layer

Peec AI is the first company in the world treating **AI-answer visibility as a measurable, competitive, multi-locale data asset**. Every other visibility tool in the room was built for one language and one market. Peec's data already spans locales. For a couple-targeted launch across three wellness cultures that barely overlap, this is not a nice-to-have — it is the compass.

**Four things only Peec gives us:**

1. **Per-locale prompt coverage.** What couples ask ChatGPT *in each market, in each language*, when something in their relationship got quiet. The German prompt set is 60% different from the US set; the Japanese set is 90% different. No other visibility tool surfaces that divergence.
2. **Per-locale citation graph.** ChatGPT's Japanese answers cite Note essays and 5ch threads. Its German answers cite *heise* and *t3n*. Its US answers cite Reddit and Wirecutter. Peec maps all three graphs. Culturally-native outreach is impossible without this.
3. **Competitive context per market.** Calm dominates English wellness. They do *not* dominate Japanese 呼吸 queries. Peec shows us exactly where incumbents are weakest per locale, which is where we attack first.
4. **Week-over-week change detection.** The AI-answer landscape shifts quarterly. Peec's longitudinal data means our 90-day plan adapts when a new entrant appears in week 6.

**Remove Peec AI and twobreath's multilingual launch has no compass.** We would pick prompts by gut feel and miss the 70% of opportunity that sits outside the English-speaking wellness ecosystem.
